## Thesis Statement

The grid was sized for the 2010s and must serve the 2030s. After twenty years of flat US power demand, three drivers compound to force the largest structural step-up since post-WWII industrialization: AI compute, industrial reshoring, and electrification of transport and heat. Consensus utility analysts model 1-2% CAGR. The true number is closer to 3-4%, with upside if AI inference scales faster than current trajectory. This is observable, not forecast — gas turbine backlogs at GE Vernova run 4-5 years, transformer lead times exceed 100 weeks, interconnect queues at every major ISO sit at multi-year highs. The decade's winners are nuclear, natural gas, and grid infrastructure.

## Why Now / Structural Drivers

1. **Demand step-change is observable, not forecast.** GE Vernova turbine backlog. Transformer lead times. Hyperscaler PPAs being signed at premium rates.
2. **Renewables hit physical limits.** Intermittency without storage is unworkable at hyperscaler reliability targets. China-controlled materials supply chain makes pure solar/wind investment a worse risk-adjusted bet than market thinks.
3. **Nuclear has political tailwind for the first time in 40 years.** Bipartisan support. Tech industry advocacy (Sam Altman, Bill Gates, Vinod Khosla). Three Mile Island restart, Diablo Canyon relicensing, Palisades reactivation.
4. **Natural gas is the bridge fuel that pundits keep declaring dead.** US LNG export capacity roughly doubles by 2030. Permian gas is associated production; midstream captures the spread.
5. **The boring-utility-to-growth-utility rerate is happening in real time.** CEG, VST, TLN have rerated significantly. Spread to laggards (regulated utilities awaiting PUC CAPEX approval) is the next leg.

## Sub-Theses

### 3.1 — Nuclear Renaissance is Real and Underpriced

Existing fleet relicensing + reactivation (Diablo Canyon, TMI restart, Palisades), SMR commercialization (NuScale, X-energy, Oklo, Kairos), and hyperscaler PPAs (CEG-Microsoft, TLN-Amazon) all signal real demand. Uranium supply has not been built up to match. Spot uranium is repricing structurally. Watch for: SMR FOAK milestones, CFPP cost curves, China's nuclear buildout pace as comparable.

### 3.2 — Natural Gas Wins the Bridge Decade

US LNG export capacity doubles by 2030. Gas turbines are sold out into 2029. The net-zero-by-2030 crowd has been wrong about gas for ten years and will be wrong for another ten. Cheniere is the merchant LNG winner. EQT/Range are the upstream Marcellus plays. Williams/OKE/KMI capture midstream spreads.

### 3.3 — Grid Infrastructure is the Unspoken Winner

Transformers, transmission, HVDC, substations, switchgear. IPPs get repriced first because they sell directly to hyperscalers. Regulated utilities follow as PUCs allow CAPEX recovery. The Hubbell/Eaton/Quanta layer is structural demand on a 10-year horizon regardless of generation mix.

### 3.4 — Renewables Play But Don't Dominate

Solar and wind continue as supplementary capacity. Battery storage matters at the margin. But the thesis is NOT a renewables thesis. Pure-play renewable equity has materials-supply-chain risk (China) and grid-integration risk that the market discounts.

## How This Could Be Wrong

AI compute could become dramatically more energy efficient (collapsing demand). Nuclear costs could blow out Vogtle-style (collapsing the SMR economic case). Regulatory throttling could choke utility growth narratives. The Trump administration's hostility to IRA subsidies whipsaws renewable adjacencies but is broadly supportive of gas and nuclear. None of these invalidate the demand step-change; they shift timing and relative winners. The biggest single risk is timing — if the rerate of CEG/VST is largely complete, the next leg requires patience.
